# Silicon-to-systems

You know how to draw logic gates. You know what a transistor is. But how do we go from a few NAND gates on a breadboard to a multi-billion transistor GPU training a massive AI model? Why did clock speeds stall in the 2000s? Why can't a CPU render graphics efficiently, and why did GPUs take over the world?

**Silicon to Systems** is a fast-paced, story-driven journey through the modern hardware stack. We explore the compute industry through the lens of history and engineering bottlenecks: every major leap in hardware happened because engineers hit a physical or logical wall and had to invent a workaround.


---

## Who is this for?

- **Prerequisites:** Basic electrical concepts and introductory digital logic. Basic architecture knowledge will make the course more interesting.
- **Format:** Reading materials, assignments and a few explore on your own topics with guidance. A final capstone project based on participation and time availability.

## Weekly Syllabus Breakdown

### Week 1 | CPU: The Foundation
*The Era of the Sequential Machine and the Memory Wall*

- **The Teaser:** *CPUs are serial at heart. What happens when your workload requires doing the same operation on two million pixels simultaneously?*

### Week 2 | GPU: The Parallel Machine
*The Era of Massive Parallelism and Spatial Compute*

- **The Teaser:** *GPUs are power-hungry, and their latency is unpredictable. What if you need a machine that responds in exact nanoseconds, every single time?*

### Week 3 | FPGA: Programmable Silicon
*The Era of Hardware-Software Co-Design*

- **The Teaser:** *FPGAs are wonderfully reconfigurable. But if your algorithm is fixed forever and you need to ship 10 million units, you are paying for flexibility you will never use.*

### Week 4 | ASIC: The Efficiency Endgame
*The Era of Custom Silicon and Domain-Specific Architectures*

- **The Future:** Post-Moore's Law survival tactics—chiplets, 3D stacking, and domain-specific accelerators.

> *\*Note: The ordering might differ*
